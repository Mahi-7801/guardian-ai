ABSTRACT The advanced capabilities in threat detection and mitigation brought about by the rapid
development of Artificial Intelligence (AI) significantly impact the fight against terrorism and cybersecurity
threats. However, critical concerns such as algorithmic bias, data quality limitations, and governance
challenges introduce significant obstacles to their deployment. This paper provides a comprehensive
overview of AI methodologies, such as predictive analytics, Natural Language Processing (NLP), and
machine learning architectures (e.g., Support V ector Machines – SVM and Long Short-Term Memory
– LSTM), and optimization algorithms (e.g., Particle Swarm Optimization – PSO), assessing their
effectiveness in security applications. Additionally, AI-based approaches to surveillance, misinformation
management, and anomaly detection are explored, focusing on their impacts on national security. Beyond the
technical aspects, the paper highlights ethical concerns and policy issues, putting forward frameworks such as
Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and crowdsourced intelligence (Crosint) to ensure the responsible
and transparent deployment of AI. The integration of the technical, ethical, and operational perspectives
addressed in this research contributes to a holistic understanding of the potential of AI in cybersecurity,
while also ensuring adherence to AI governance standards.

Learning (RL) [1], which significantly improve the ability to
analyze large volumes of data, metadata, online archives, and
social networking activity. This drives the security agencies
to identify individuals and potential terrorist threats [2].
Military and defense applications enhance the military’s
ability to respond to threats and maintain national security

C. RESEARCH GAP & MOTIVATION
In recent years, the academic community has extensively
explored the potential of AI in cybersecurity. The techniques
that have been mentioned so far and those that will follow
in the paper offer improved capabilities in threat detection,
malware detection, and so on. However, newer techniques
will have to be explored and developed beyond the usual
ones in order to deal effectively with new types of threats.
IoT applications supported by AA Vs will need to rely on
modern and sophisticated Artificial Intelligence algorithms to
successfully address contemporary challenges [14]. Human
collaboration with intelligence-generating AI systems is
critical to ensure the provision of knowledge and analysis
beyond human capabilities, leading to more effective threat
detection and response strategies [15].
The use of machine learning, Deep Learning, and Natural
Language Processing (NLP) is an area that is constantly
evolving and being researched as they are proven to improve
the accuracy and speed of threat detection and response [16].
To ensure the above, the development of integrated frame-
works with protocols specifically designed for AI that
address the ethical and regulatory challenges associated with
AI is essential. Furthermore, encouraging interdisciplinary
collaboration will promote the development of innovative
security solutions that integrate knowledge from a variety
of disciplines, not necessarily technical [17]. A significant
issue is the development of robust AI systems to prevent
adversarial AI threats in both cybersecurity and counter-
terrorism applications. Current methods like the RADAR
framework [18] improve the robustness of cyber defense
systems, but there is a need to continue the development
of comprehensive and innovative methodologies to address
these threats and create more effective defenses. An important
area for future research, particularly in the phases of
design, development, and deployment of AI systems [19],
is Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI), which aims
to enhance the understanding of the decisions made by
models incorporating AI techniques and methods [20].
By providing transparency and interpretability in AI models,
mutual trust between operators and analysts, and systems is
achieved [21], significantly increasing the effectiveness of
security operations [22].
D. CHALLENGES IN AI-DRIVEN SECURITY SYSTEMS
Artificial Intelligence systems are not foolproof. On the
contrary, they are vulnerable to various security threats at
every stage of the process, including data collection, training,
and deployment [23].
The most common attacks involve sensor tampering,
poisoning, and data quality corruption by adversarial attacks,
putting the reliability and integrity of the models at seri-
ous risk [24]. The complexity and diversity of data and
AI technologies prevent the standardized development of
security models and practices. In addition, the need to
comply with regulatory requirements that vary from country
to country makes it difficult to a significant extent to develop
effective models as strong safety measures are required for
AI systems [25].
Another important challenge of many AI models is
their opaque model nature, making it difficult for users
to understand and trust their decisions. Current methods
limit the provision of clear explanations, negatively affecting
decision-making and crisis management, making the systems
vulnerable to attack [26].
A particularly critical technical issue is adversarial attacks,
especially in cybersecurity applications [27]. The speci-
ficity of the problem lies in the fact that attackers are
constantly adapting their strategies using AI to enhance
the effectiveness of attacks and lead to the manipulation
and deception of AI-based security systems [28]. Another
significant challenge is ransomware detection. Machine
learning algorithms are increasingly being used to detect
ransomware by exploiting the ability to handle large amounts
of data and analyzing behavioral patterns [29]. Examples of
successful and highly accurate detection are the End-to-End
Ransomware Detection System (E2E-RDS) frameworks and
the AIRaD tool [30]. However, one of the most important
challenges in AI-based ransomware detection is the ability to
adapt to new variants of these threats[31] and reduce the false
positive rates produced by security systems [32]. Through
the analysis of the behavior and mechanisms of ransomware
attacks, it is possible to identify trends in the way they evolve
and, using machine learning models, to detect and mitigate
the consequences [33].

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is widely investigated in the
context of counter-terrorism and cybersecurity, and several
studies focus on its role in predictive threat analysis, early
intrusion detection, and disinformation mitigation. However,
although most studies contribute significantly to the field by
thoroughly analyzing AI-based security methodologies, most
of them focus on the technical aspects rather than providing a
comprehensive and integrated view of the applications of AI
across the broader national and cybersecurity landscape. This
section highlights the differences between the present study
and existing research by providing a review of previous work.
The use of Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning
(DL) has been widely explored in the context of research
on their application to cybersecurity, anomaly and intrusion

C. PREDICTIVE FRAMEWORKS, THREAT DETECTION, AND
STRATEGIC PLANNING
The contribution of Artificial Intelligence to counter-
terrorism efforts aims to enhance predictive accuracy, threat
detection, and strategic planning. Such technologies work
together through supervised learning methods, deep learning,
machine learning, data mining, and other methods and
techniques to detect and analyze extremist content of large
volumes of data leading to the identification of possible
terrorist activities online [48]. A typical example of of
accurate predictive evaluation was the results of a model
trained on data from 1970 to 2015 that predicts new terrorist
attack locations at a rate of 96.6% [69].
AI-based predictive models and methodologies attempt
to achieve more accurate predictions of terrorist activities
such as the combination of Support V ector Machine (SVM)
and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) algorithms, combined
in ensemble machine learning models, which yield high
accuracy rates in predicting areas prone to terrorist activities,
thus enhancing efforts to combat terrorism[70]. Furthermore,
by exploiting the computational capabilities of Particle
Swarm Optimization (PSO) meta-heuristic algorithms, the
optimal weight assignments for the key learners, namely
Random Forest and Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost),
are determined [55] to process structured, semi-structured,
and unstructured data features aiming to predict and mitigate
these incidents [71].
Additionally, techniques such as unsupervised machine
learning, exploratory data analysis and statistical test-
ing are used to discover features that evaluate terrorist
motives [6]. Another deep learning architecture, used to
manage terrorism-related behavioral patterns and optimize
the evaluation model through convolutional neural networks,
determines the risk level of suspected individuals, applies
‘Top-k’ selection [72]. An important application of deep
neural networks related to the automatic recognition of
individuals and objects, the retrieval of mental speech, and
the analysis of desensitized behavior emerges from research
conducted at the CAMPUS research center, the University
‘‘Politehnica’’ of Bucharest. Such Artificial Intelligence
systems have the potential to process huge amounts of data
from surveillance cameras, satellite data, drones, and other
portable recording devices. The results provide human-level
accuracy in the field of identification and detection of
potential threats [54].
Furthermore, using reinforcement learning algorithms
such as GCPDDQN enables the successful countering of
armed autonomous clusters of autonomous aerial vehicles
(AA Vs). The GCPDDQN algorithm decays and disrupts
cluster communications, thus neutralizing threats [38], [57].
Another application of Artificial Intelligence and machine
learning is in enhancing the wireless connectivity of
Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) and the Internet of Things,
with techniques such as transfer learning, and distributed
and federated learning. This process is particularly critical
as it ensures the seamless collection and analysis of data in
real-time during counter-terrorism operations, improving the
effectiveness and responsiveness of systems [73].
One of the techniques used in extremism detection is
Natural Language Processing (NLP), where a collection
of techniques allows systems to understand the natural,
unprocessed language that people use to communicate [74].
NLP techniques detect suspicious patterns and semantic
elements within texts, as models such as Naive Bayes,
Logistic Regression, or Support V ector Machines (SVM),
are trained on data to categorize texts as legitimate or
phishing. Features such as email metadata, message content,
URL structures, and keywords help to distinguish them [75].
Monitoring and analysis of social media content is of
key importance since detailed recording and processing of
extracted data helps to identify potential recruitment and
propaganda activities by terrorist groups [76]. NLP and topic
modeling techniques, powered by machine learning, are also
used to understand the motivations of terrorist groups that
categorize targets through narrative texts [55]. Furthermore,
by using data from social networks such as tweets and
messages, radical, extremist, and terrorist content is identified

D. ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE TECHNIQUES IN
CYBERSECURITY
Undoubtedly, in modern cybersecurity environments, the role
of Artificial Intelligence is becoming increasingly necessary.
From a technical point of view, statistical models, such as
Bayesian networks, which model probabilistic dependencies,
are used to detect and counter threats in real-time. First,
they automate the retrieval of knowledge from previous
studies, and then systematize the course of action taken by
the attackers in a framework. This enhances the efforts of
security analysts to more easily identify the most influential
actors in a global threat [81]. The prediction of potential
cybersecurity attacks and breaches of organizations’ security
systems can be based on historical data and emerging trends,
and in the context of smart cities, the possibilities offered by
the integration of Artificial Intelligence at the cybersecurity
level significantly enhance the resilience of cities against
digital threats [82].
Enhancing cybersecurity includes applications of Artificial
Intelligence with machine learning algorithms, decision trees,
neural networks, and support vector machines [40], as well
as the development of hybrid systems that leverage deep
learning, through multi-agent systems, to help mitigate
cyber-attacks on critical infrastructure, protecting critical
assets while maintaining organizations’ trust in digital
ecosystems [83]. Furthermore, machine learning algorithms
identify suspicious activities of users and entities in a network
by analyzing their behavior. Identifying abnormal or high-
risk behaviors, which may indicate a cyber-attack, reduces
the risk of data and system breaches and enhances the overall
security infrastructure of an organization [84]. Such systems,
known as Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS), are believed to
be able to fill the gap in traditional security methods as they
can detect different threats and attacks in cyberspace and can
respond in real-time [16], [56].
These techniques provide advanced capabilities for threat
detection, process automation such as incident response,
and predictive analysis, helping to mitigate cyber threats
in cyberspace from terrorist activities. This allows security
systems to analyze vast amounts of data in real time and
detect early indications of malicious activities and threatening
events [85]. At the same time, recent research findings
propose a new two-stage deep learning technique based on
the hybridization of Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) and
Auto-Encoders (AE) for accurate and timely detection of
network intrusions [86]. Artificial neural network (ANN)
algorithms demonstrate significant effectiveness in detecting
security breaches and unwanted messages, thus protecting
systems from vulnerabilities and data from potential loss[87].
Moreover, automation of daily security tasks reduces human
intervention, results in less human overhead, and minimizes
human errors, thus improving overall performance and
response times.
Enhancing the transparency and interpretability of cyber-
security systems is a critical issue of particular importance
for reducing false positives [36]. This can be addressed by
Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI), which provides
security analysts with the understanding necessary to lead
to more effective decision making. V arious XAI methods
and techniques, including LIME, SHAP [42], MetaCluster,
and TRUST [44], are used to make machine learning
models more interpretable by providing feature importance
scores and interpretable reports that support decision makers.
Furthermore, in contexts such as those of IoT, XAI enhances
intrusion detection systems (IDS) by detecting anoma-
lies [22], while addressing the opaque nature of AI models
by increasing the trust and confidence of system users [41].
The increasing role of XAI in improving interpretability
is critical in different domains where decision bias is
crucial [53], [88].
A particularly beneficial technology is generative Artificial
Intelligence. Its novelty lies in the fact that this form of
Artificial Intelligence creates synthetic data, in addition to
existing data, thus helping to identify threats from different

actors [89]. This technology is crucial for Security Operations

Large Language Models (LLMs) contribute significantly

applications such as threat detection, phishing detection,

in the field of counter-terrorism and national security pro-

detection and response by automating complex tasks without
direct supervision, and combining multiple AI techniques to
deliver robust and accurate cybersecurity outcomes, improv-
ing the effectiveness of cybersecurity operations[95]. Despite
the numerous advantages that Artificial Intelligence offers for
enhancing cybersecurity systems, significant challenges such
as technical limitations and ethical concerns are emerging.

E. CHALLENGES AND LIMITATIONS OF AI IN
CYBERSECURITY
The ever-increasing frequency of attacks and threats in
cyberspace makes the use of Artificial Intelligence in cyber-
security systems a necessity; however, apart from the obvious
benefits derived from its use in combating terrorism and

reliably identify threats through mass surveillance, as issues

false alarms and a degradation of the reliability of threat
detection systems [100].
A particularly critical issue is the attacks on AI through

The impact of such attacks on fragile and vulnerable systems

healthcare, autonomous vehicles, and IoT systems [103] are
in the spotlight.
Other critical constraints are the lack of sufficient data

G. SENTIMENT ANAL YSIS FOR THREAT DETECTION AND
HATE SPEECH
Sentiment analysis plays an important role in the identifi-
cation and prevention of extremist behavior, as it provides
information on the emotional state of internet users and
predicts expressions often associated with radicalization.
Emotions such as hatred and resistance reinforce and
shape the extremist profile of users and provide clear
indications of offending activities [121]. Thus, in the context
of counter-terrorism and intelligence gathering, sentiment
analysis can be used to monitor social media, forums, or other
types of online channels [122]. Moreover, the rapid spread
and use of social networking media exacerbate the spread
of hate speech. Therefore, sophisticated tools are needed
to detect and mitigate the negative consequences of online
aggression.
V arious studies propose a variety of methodologies and
techniques, such as parallelized versions of bagging, A-
stacking, and random subspace algorithms, which demon-
strate high success rates and accuracy, especially when the
data set to be processed is particularly large [123]. Similarly,
techniques such as Natural Language Processing (NLP) allow
for the automated processing of large volumes of data on web
platforms by efficiently identifying rhetorical content [124].
Furthermore, for classifying harmful content and predicting
rhetorical hate speech in social networking media, the use of
attentional multi-channel convolutional-BiLSTM networks
that combine word representation techniques, multi-channel
convolution, and bidirectional LSTM cells is proposed [125].
Another technique is to use HyperNetworks at a feature level
that is smaller in size than other popular pre-trained language
models [126], while another half-speech detection model,
pattern-based speech recognition (PDHS), uses pre-trained
language models and aspects such as term frequency, labels
of parts of speech, and scores from sentiment analysis [127].
An interesting approach proposes the use of translations
and paraphrases for rhetorical misinformation detection,
with the main idea being to improve the processed dataset,
offering training in a diverse and integrated dataset [128].
Furthermore, the application of traditional algorithms, such
as recurrent neural networks (RNN) and BERT-based models,
is effective in detecting racist and xenophobic hate speech
online [129]. A hybrid analysis approach based on dictionary
and ML techniques, such as Decision Tree, Naive Bayes,
and Support V ector Machine, contributes to the identification
of excessive sentiment, i.e. early signs of unrest or other
potential threats to public and national security [130].

H. PROPOSED FRAMEWORK FOR ENHANCING
SENTIMENT ANAL YSIS IN COUNTER-TERRORISM
Modern sentiment analysis techniques, although often
focused on real-time data, contribute effectively to efforts
to identify extremist behavior and hate speech online [136].
However, the analysis of instantaneous data significantly
limits the ability of models to capture the dynamic nature of
rapidly evolving emotions that arise in response to changing
conditions and situations, such as political instability, various
types of social unrest, and economic pressures.
A solution to these issues may be found by integrating
temporal, spatial, and socio-political variables into existing
techniques and methods of sentiment analysis. This, in turn,
will improve the accuracy of predictive analysis by taking
into account the way in which emotions change over time,
location, and the wider socio-political context, leading to
a more holistic approach to the process. More specifically,
temporal analysis captures the way in which emotions change
over time, spatial analysis links specific geographical regions
to emotions, and socio-political context analysis accounts for
variables such as news events and political dynamics.
In summary, the hypothesis that negative sentiment in
a region escalates during periods of political instability or
social unrest, combined with the integration of real-time data
streams from social networking media, news sources and
different types of platforms, can provide a more complete pic-
ture of the factors leading, first to social destabilization and
radicalization, and then to extremist and terrorist activity. The
application of the above during the critical phase of machine
learning algorithm design will enhance the capacity for better
decision-making, particularly in areas where rapidly evolving
environments require more accurate predictions of potential
extremist behavior, and which traditional sentiment analysis
can overlook.

I. NETWORK ANAL YSIS IN THE IDENTIFICATION OF

Equally important and closely related to sentiment analysis,
which provides important information about the behavior and
rhetoric of individuals, is the examination of the way in which

analysis is a critical stage in understanding and identifying
extremist groups, as it provides important information about
their structure, communication patterns, and overall dynam-
ics, and is instrumental in mapping groups, detecting the core
of the network, and clarifying the roles of key individuals
within it [137]. This approach is particularly effective in
online environments, where identifying key players and
understanding organizational structure and influences is
crucial for countering and deconstructing extremist networks.
Artificial Intelligence contributes significantly to network
analysis. Different theories, such as that of social learning,
can contribute to a better understanding of the mechanism of
radicalization of the members of a group [138]. But also on a
practical level, the mapping of relationships between entities
related to terrorism can be achieved by using neural networks,
in particular the Bert-A TT model, which extracts efficient
graphs [58]. Another effective technique used to translate
the risk of spreading terrorism is the Spatiotemporal Graph
Convolutional Network (STGCN). Indeed, when combined
with LSTM networks, it has achieved high rates of prediction
and predictive analysis [139]. In addition, techniques such
as the unsupervised extreme situation detection method,

L. MISINFORMATION AND DISINFORMATION IN
INTELLIGENCE GATHERING
When gathering information, especially in the digital age,
where data is disseminated rapidly and in large volumes
VOLUME 13, 2025 91753
--- Page 15 ---
I. Syllaidopoulos et al.: Comprehensive Survey on AI in Counter-Terrorism and Cybersecurity
through web-based applications, attention should be focused
on avoiding the acquisition of incorrect information and
on countering misinformation. In this way, confusion and
distorted decisions will be avoided, especially where there is a
risk of causing serious malfunctions during counter-terrorism
operations.
Incorrect information usually comes from individuals who
unknowingly share inaccurate or misleading information.
Conversely, misinformation is mainly used by actors, such
as terrorists and extremist groups, to disrupt intelligence
gathering processes and thus weaken national security [185].
It has also been observed that terrorist groups take advantage
of the advances in technology and the wide reach of
social networking media, and engage in disinformation
campaigns and psychological operations, with the aim of
manipulating the perceptions of unsuspecting citizens [186].
It is a frequent phenomenon that different groups use false
narratives that mislead and discredit attempts to distinguish
between accurate and false information. They use techniques
and methods such as creating sockpuppet accounts [187] on
social networking sites, copying and paraphrasing content
from various platforms and news feeds, and often succeed in
creating a sense of credibility [51].
Thus, the phenomenon of legitimization of information is
observed, which plays a decisive role, especially in times
of crisis, where the distinction between truth and falsehood
is often blurred and indistinguishable. Indeed, it has been
observed that paranoid content is republished through social
networking media and leads to social polarization and,
in extreme situations, to radicalization [188]. Under this
prism, the dual role of AI is clear, as it is used both as a tool
to identify and combat misinformation and as a tool for its
dissemination [189].
To detect misinformation, in real time, on social net-
working platforms, Artificial Intelligence tools using Natural
Language Processing (NLP) techniques are used [46].
In combination with deep learning models such as Convolu-
tional Neural Networks (CNN), Recurrent Neural Networks
(RNN), and transformer-based models such as BERT and
GPT, have shown significant benefits in the fight against
misinformation [49]. Furthermore, to improve the accuracy
in detecting misinformation, hybrid deep learning models
combined with pre-trained text models such as Global
V ectors (GloV e) and Universal Sentence Encoders (USE) are
proposed [47].
Of particular interest are automated event control systems,
Human-in-the-Loop, which exploit both the capabilities of
Artificial Intelligence and human judgment, and in this
combination detect misinformation with high accuracy, once
they are able to understand and perceive the context in a
sentence [190].
In addition to detecting content with misinformation,
an important activity is to reduce its propagation. Thus,
algorithms based on matrix factorization (MF) are proposed;
through this technique, annotations are selected from user
postings and publications [191].
Similarly, improved versions of models such as BERT
and Roberta, which detect content generated by Artificial
Intelligence through LLMs—a phenomenon that is becoming
more and more common and complex [72]—provide a partic-
ular contribution to reducing the spread of misinformation.
One such case is FakeBert, based on BERT, and combin-
ing convolutional neural networks (CNN). This approach
captures semantic dependencies bidirectionally, allows for
a better understanding of the content, and maximizes the
probabilities for security systems to detect misinformation

IV. ETHICAL, LEGAL, AND GOVERNANCE
CONSIDERATIONS
The present research has highlighted the obvious advan-
tages arising from the development and use of Artificial
Intelligence in various areas of security, including the
fight against terrorism, extremism, and manifestations of
these phenomena [198]. Although the benefits are obvious,
at the same time, significant ethical concerns and major
privacy risks arise. This is because systems incorporating
AI techniques and methods are often operated without the
explicit consent of the individuals being monitored; therefore,
the boundaries between necessary security measures and
privacy violations are blurred [37]. Furthermore, systematic
biases embedded in training datasets [199] undermine the
accountability of these systems [200].
Under these conditions, the application of appropriate
design techniques for such systems as well as the develop-
ment of relevant frameworks, such as Explainable Artificial
Intelligence (XAI) [201], can effectively address such
phenomena. With a focus on promoting transparency, they
will help software designers to understand the environment in
which AI decisions are based. Furthermore, by encouraging
international cooperation, establishing rules that mitigate the
ethical implications of the use of advanced AI technologies,
and involving more and more stakeholders, the aim of
raising awareness and common decision-making will be
achieved [60].
A. STATE LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK
The importance of developing initiatives in order to take
common decisions on the regulations related to the use of
AI can be understood by examining the existing legislative
framework that defines and regulates these regulations.
Although they vary and are constantly evolving, there are
significant gaps, and this lack of a single global standard,
mainly due to the transnational nature of these technologies,
leads to inconsistencies in safety measures [63].
In the EU, a step has recently been taken towards the
creation of comprehensive legal frameworks. The AI Act
attempts to express the EU’s overall digital strategy, with
the protection of citizens’ fundamental rights as a key
approach [64]. The categorization of AI systems character-
izes the AI Act according to their level of risk [202], and lays
the foundations for the global governance of AI, with clear
influences and using elements from the implementation of the
General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Furthermore,
EU AI law innovates by introducing regulatory ‘‘sandboxes’’,
so that the testing and development of such technologies can
take place in a controlled environment in order to comply with
all the established rules [203].
On the contrary, the United States prefers a soft adaptation
of existing legal frameworks, rather than the creation of new
ones, resulting in an uneven regulatory environment, while
China has opted for a more centralized regulatory framework,
integrating AI into public life with a focus on control and
surveillance [204].
B. GOVERNANCE AND AI
Although in different directions, the interest of major states
in establishing rules to ensure compliance with legal and
ethical standards in the use of AI is real, but requires the
development of governance frameworks that ensure oversight
and continuous regulation and adaptation. This will both
minimize the risks to public and national security, and
ensure respect for and protection of human rights [62].
To counter the threats of competitive AI and to safeguard
the principles of Explainable Artificial Intelligence [43],
a series of protective measures must be taken, especially
in vulnerable AI systems [205], and to strengthen, through
strong model training, the defense mechanisms. In addition,
it is right to strike a balance between safety and explainability
in order to avoid phenomena in which AI goes beyond the

C. CHALLENGES, CONSTRAINTS, AND PROPOSALS
It is obvious that in order to strike a balance between security
and privacy, the policymakers are faced with a multifaceted
set of challenges. The need to ensure algorithmic trans-
parency, accountability, and the acquisition and maintenance
of strategic advantages against threats to public and national
security is pushing information services to rapidly adopt and
integrate AI into their existing security systems [206].
The main challenge, as mentioned earlier, is the invasion
of privacy, as their effective operation requires resources and
huge amounts of personal data. Thus, the potential lack of
transparency and accountability further exacerbates concerns
about holding security systems accountable for the actions
and processes followed in making a decision [65].
Another important challenge is the rapid pace of devel-
opment of Artificial Intelligence, as it makes it difficult for
the authorities responsible for establishing legal frameworks
to keep up, with the result that regulations appear outdated
and inadequate. Furthermore, the use and integration of AI
into technologies such as facial recognition has been subject
to considerable criticism, as a number of studies, such as
Clearview AI and Rite Aid, have shown that there have been
significant privacy violations [207].

VI. CHALLENGES AND OPEN ISSUES
Despite the significant progress made by the integration of
Artificial Intelligence (AI) in the security systems tasked
with counter-terrorism and cybersecurity, critical challenges
remain in the technical, ethical, and operational domains.
In summary, the main challenges and issues that need to be
addressed in order to ensure the responsible and effective
deployment of AI in security systems are the following:
• Algorithmic bias and vulnerabilities, as algorithms
remain vulnerable to attacks by adversaries and affect
the accuracy of decision making.
• The quality and heterogeneity of the data sets
(HUMINT, SIGINT, OSINT) are at risk of misinforma-
tion and manipulation.
• Real-time processing, especially in threat detection,
faces significant limitations.
• Massive and unmonitored surveillance and moni-
toring raise concerns about invasion of privacy and
circumvention of regulatory frameworks.
• The limited use of explainable AI (XAI) in models
and algorithms is an inhibiting factor in transparency in
decision making.
• Significant divergences are observed in AI governance
frameworks between states.
• Organizational reluctance to implement AI-based
solutions makes it difficult to build trust and provide
specialized training for their staff.
• There is a risk that rival entities may exploit
crowd-sourced intelligence streams, and through imi-
tation and propaganda, increase the risk of misinforma-
tion.
• Cybersecurity preparedness gaps are widened by
the lack of resources needed to implement advanced
AI-based security frameworks.

VII. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This paper has presented the opportunities and possibilities
offered by the integration of AI in the fight against terrorism
and cybersecurity. Improving intelligence gathering pro-
cesses enhances security frameworks and mitigates emerging
threats. Through a comprehensive analysis of AI method-
ologies, such as predictive analytics, Natural Language
Processing (NLP), and Deep Learning models, the ethical and
governance challenges that need to be addressed in order to
develop AI in a responsible manner were highlighted.
One of the main conclusions of this study is that security
systems developed based on AI must be combined with
commitments to ethical principles, operational transparency,
and international cooperation. Furthermore, ensuring the
effectiveness of AI requires strong governance frameworks
that ensure fairness, accountability, and trust. This will
eliminate the risks of invasion of privacy, abuse by malicious
actors and algorithmic bias and ensure the responsible
development of AI.
It is recognized that extremist activities and threats in
cyberspace cross national borders, which is why collaborative
efforts between governments, agencies, and academic insti-
tutions can harmonize regulatory frameworks, encourage
